01 · Research goal
How can an interactive learning experience help users recognize the connection between their short-video behavior, algorithmic recommendations, and consumer influence?

I wanted to understand how college students experience short-video recommendation systems and whether they notice how their own actions shape the feed over time.

This project began as a course-based concept. After the semester ended, I independently rebuilt the project website and prototype, using the original research direction as the foundation.

03 · Key findings

Key Findings

The research pointed to three gaps that shaped the prototype.

01

Users know algorithms exist, but not how they train the feed.

Students often describe the feed as something that simply “knows what they like,” rather than as a system shaped by their own actions.

Why it matters

The design needed to connect everyday actions to recommendation patterns.

02

Repeated exposure makes consumer content feel natural.

Product videos blend into lifestyle content, trends, routines, and casual recommendations, so users may not recognize them as persuasive.

Why it matters

The experience needed to show how persuasion develops gradually through repetition.

03

Users experience personalization as personal preference.

Students often interpret repeated content as a reflection of their own taste, which can hide the role of algorithmic reinforcement.

Why it matters

The prototype needed to make the feedback loop between action and system response visible.

04 · Design implications

From Research to Design

Each finding was translated into a prototype decision.

Research insight Users do not fully understand how their actions train the feed.
→
Design decision Let users interact with content before explaining the algorithm.
Research insight Algorithmic influence builds gradually over time.
→
Design decision Show feed changes across multiple rounds instead of one static explanation.
Research insight Product content often blends into entertainment.
→
Design decision Use lifestyle, influencer, and trend-based content—not obvious ads only.
Research insight Users experience personalization as personal preference.
→
Design decision Reveal how their actions shaped the simulated feed.
Research insight Students dislike being lectured about their media habits.
→
Design decision Use simulation and reflection instead of passive explanation.
05 · Audience

Target Learner

A learner profile synthesized from interview patterns and the project context.

Learner persona

Emma, 20

College student / Daily short-video user

Emma uses short-video platforms for entertainment, stress relief, trends, and product discovery. She enjoys content that feels personal, but sometimes buys items after seeing them repeatedly.

She knows algorithms exist, but does not always understand how likes, skips, saves, and watch time shape what appears next.

Entertainment Stress relief Trends Product discovery

Pain Points

  • Notices repeated products but does not know why they keep appearing
  • Struggles to separate genuine interest from repetition
  • Sometimes regrets purchases influenced by repeated exposure
  • Assumes a product is popular because “everyone has it”

Learner Needs

  • Understand how recommendations respond to behavior
  • Recognize persuasive consumer content
  • Notice repeated exposure patterns
  • Reflect on how scrolling shapes preferences
  • Build one realistic strategy for intentional consumption
Research direction

Make the feedback loop visible.

The research led to a simulation built around action, pattern recognition, and reflection.
