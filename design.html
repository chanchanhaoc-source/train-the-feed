Train the Feed · Design

Design.

Turning research insights into an interactive learning experience that helps users see how their actions train the feed.

The challenge was to make an invisible feedback loop visible, personal, and easy to reflect on.

01 · Design challenge
How might we help college students recognize how their short-video actions shape future recommendations and influence consumer decisions over time?

Students often know algorithms exist, but do not clearly see how their own behaviors train the feed. Instead of another passive explainer, I designed around action, pattern recognition, and reflection.

01

Make it experiential

Learners should learn by interacting, not only reading.

02

Make it personal

The experience should respond to user choices.

03

Make it reflective

The simulation should connect to real scrolling habits.

02 · Principles

Design Principles

I translated the research findings into four principles that guided the prototype direction.

01

Experience before explanation

Learners interact with a simulated feed before seeing how their actions shaped recommendations.

02

Make the invisible loop visible

Reveal the connection between action, recommendation change, repeated exposure, and reflection.

03

Show persuasion as gradual

Consumer influence appears through lifestyle, influencer, and trend content—not only obvious ads.

04

Reflect without blaming

Help learners notice patterns and make intentional choices without telling them they were manipulated.

03 · Outcomes

Learning Goals

The learning goals focused on awareness, recognition, reflection, and intentional action.

Goal 01

Algorithmic Awareness

Understand how recommendations respond to user behavior.

Goal 02

Persuasive Strategy Recognition

Recognize persuasive consumer strategies in short videos.

Goal 03

Reflective Digital Consumption

Reflect on how repetition may shape preferences and purchases.

Goal 04

Intentional Strategy Building

Create one strategy for more intentional digital consumption.

Informed by Bloom’s and Fink’s taxonomies, emphasizing understanding, application, reflection, and self-directed learning.

04 · Exploration

Concept Development

I compared three ways to teach algorithm awareness before selecting the simulated feed.

Idea 01

Algorithm Explainer

A text-based explanation could define recommendation systems, but felt too passive.

Idea 02

Persuasive Content Analysis

Users could identify influencer marketing, but not see recommendations changing over time.

Idea 03

Simulated Short-Video Feed

Learners experience the feedback loop directly through choices, repetition, and reflection.

05 · Final concept

Train the Feed

A simulated short-video experience where learners interact with content, notice recommendation patterns, and reflect on how behavior can shape future feeds and consumer decisions.

07 · Decisions

Design Rationale

The key decisions made algorithmic influence experiential, visible, and reflective.

01

Simulation over explanation

A simulated feed makes the feedback loop easier to feel than text alone.

02

Loop over linear lesson

The experience mirrors action, recommendation shift, repetition, and reflection.

03

Organic content over obvious ads

Lifestyle, trend, influencer, and product content show how persuasion blends into entertainment.

04

Reflection over judgment

Guided prompts help learners notice patterns without feeling blamed.
